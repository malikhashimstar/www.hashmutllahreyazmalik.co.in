01 — Who I Am

Not achievements.
But questions.

My name is Hashmatullah Reyaz Malik. I come from Kashmir — a place that teaches you early how to carry something without showing it. I was born into a body that moves differently through the world: I live with Osteogenesis Imperfecta Type III, and I use a wheelchair. It has not defined me — but it has deepened me.

I do not write from comfort. I write from the edges of understanding — from the place between what I feel and what I can explain. Most of what I know, I learned not from certainty but from watching things fall apart and studying the shape of the wreckage.

There is something I have always believed: that a person who pays attention to life — truly pays attention — will find more wisdom in a single Tuesday than a textbook offers in a year. I have tried to be that person. I am still trying.

I write sometimes. I think about the cosmos sometimes. Both are the same gesture — a mind reaching outward before the moment disappears.

Sisyphus

by Hashmatullah Reyaz Malik

At its quiet center, this work traces two distant lives.

A boy who has never been fully seen—moving through the edges of his world, defined more by absence than presence.
And a girl who has been seen too much—reduced, misread, and left searching for something beyond the surface that defines her.

They meet in a space where identity is fluid and distance feels negotiable. What begins as an exchange of thought slowly unfolds into something neither of them fully understands.

This project explores what it means to be known, and what it costs to be misunderstood. It is less concerned with answers, and more with the fragile, often invisible threads that draw people together—and the forces that quietly pull them apart.

Still evolving, it remains a work shaped by questions rather than conclusions. It is currently under development and will be released shortly.

In Development

Valerius

by Hashmatullah Reyaz Malik

History is not a straight line; it is a relentless loop. Valerius explores this terrifying repetition.

It is the story of human nature—how a man is often told he should not overthink, that he should simply act, because deep in his blood, he has always been a conqueror. He is built to fight, to build, to take what is his, and to ignore the heavy questions that slow him down.

But what is the price of that blind conquest? When a man stops thinking and only conquers, he merely reconstructs the same ruins of the past. He becomes a prisoner to his own triumphs, repeating the same cycles of war, ambition, and inevitable collapse.

This book is a deep exploration of the conqueror's paradox: fighting so desperately hard to build an empire within oneself or the world, only to realize that history is just using you to repeat its oldest tragedies.

Currently in its conceptual stages, delving into the philosophy of ambition and the cycles of human history.

In Development

The Cold Hearth

by Hashmatullah Reyaz Malik

There is a specific kind of freezing that happens not in the winter air, but inside the chest when a place that once felt like home no longer does.

The Cold Hearth is a deep dive into the profound isolation of outgrowing the places and people that once kept us safe. It tells the story of what happens after the love is gone, after the companions have left the fire, and only the cold ashes remain.

It is about the empty rooms we carry inside our minds—the echoes of conversations that will never happen again, and the chilling realization that 'home' is no longer a physical place, but a memory we cannot return to.

This narrative explores the grief of moving on. It asks the painful question: How do we build a new fire when our hands are still covered in the ash of the old one?

A quiet meditation on grief, survival, and the cold spaces left behind. Currently under development.

Author’s Note

Most of what you’ll read here has come out of observation and dedication—because without them, nothing real ever comes into words. Writing doesn’t arrive out of comfort; it forms when something unsettles you, when it sinks deep enough into your bones that silence is no longer an option.

Even with a practical understanding of how the world works, there are parts of me that still long for the very things I write about. This, perhaps, is my way of letting go—by giving those thoughts a place to exist outside of me.

This website is not an attempt to impress. It is simply an attempt to connect—with anyone who has ever felt something similar but couldn’t quite put it into words.

So don’t read this as writings alone. Read it as reflection. If something resonates, pause for a moment and ask yourself where you stand within it.

— Hashmatullah Reyaz Malik

Literary Essay  ·  Existential Prose

Limerent Misery

by Hashmatullah Reyaz Malik

Chapter I — Why Do We Suffer?

Why do you think we suffer? Look around you. There are people you see in a constant state of suffering, and you hope that their pain will end. You might even paint a picture in your mind that this will be over soon, but it never is for them. Life keeps giving them shocks, like earthquakes happening on an intermediate level. For a moment there is happiness, and suddenly everything is quiet. You may feel that their life will eventually be good, because you are not seeing what they are truly going through. For them, it has always been hard. I knew a person, and I thought exactly that—that his suffering was just a brief moment, and eventually, the universe would reward him with something he had never even imagined. I always held onto the mindset that there are ups and downs, and eventually, you find the stability you always wanted. At least, that is what I thought.

And then this person I knew died. Just a few days before his death, I met him, and we talked about life. I asked him how things were going, and he wearily said a few words: "Nothing is going well." I dared to ask him more about how his life had been, and I learned so much. But at the time, the only thing in my mind—the only thing I held onto—was the belief that it was going to end well. I thought he wouldn't suffer for life. I thought he would eventually find peace and stability. That thought kept me happy, even after knowing he was suffering. Because of my belief that everything would eventually be okay, I told myself not to worry, that it was just temporary.

And then the worst happened. My belief lost all its meaning. No matter how I thought about him, it wasn't going to end well because he was no longer there to see the change. His life had perished into billions of scattered ashes, and my belief remained stagnant, entirely useless.

Without diving deeply into who he was, I must say he was a good man. To me, he deserved the change. He deserved to be happy and peaceful. I don't know what the criteria are for being eligible for a peaceful life, but if it is about being good, then he surely qualified. But guess what? We don't know what Allah has written for us. My complaint isn't with God; it is that we simply don't understand the reality of life. It isn't that God is inept, but rather that we take life so for granted and treat it so casually.

I am not denying that there is balance for some people. For some, life is a steady wave—they go through ups and downs and eventually find stability. But you know what I call them? I call them lucky. Because even after all that disruption, their life still has a meaningful end to chase. But we can't deny the fact that there are people who are not as lucky. When I came to the realization that life could be worse, I became thankful to God that at least it wasn't me. But who knows what is coming? In my personal philosophy, I believe we can never guess what awaits us. You might be working your hardest, expecting your life to become a luxurious, stable one, but things change. They sometimes take turns so vigorously that nothing is left for your end. I have seen so many examples of how life turned out for people. It is simply hard.

Chapter II — The Anatomy of Isolation

And then there is the misery of isolation. The lack of belongingness, the lack of being seen, the inability to show the world your true capabilities—this is a profound part of misery. A life becomes miserable, completely losing its meaning, when it lacks the very things needed to survive: a seed of love, the chance to plant a seed of hope in someone else.

Now, I remember the deadline. I remember Frankenstein. What do you think would be his biggest misery if Frankenstein's monster were real? Is it that he is a monster? Is it that he cannot die? No. It is that he does not have a companion. And he will never have a companion. He was assembled from pieces of men who always wanted to be loved, men who died in wars fighting for their country, feeling profoundly lonely. His longing, his desperate want for love, was so overwhelming that he couldn't hide his feelings from his creator—yet his creator loathed him because of what he was.

This is the essence of limerent misery. It is the agonizing paradox of possessing a heart capable of bottomless devotion, only to realize there is nowhere to put it down. It is standing in a crowded room, completely invisible to the one pair of eyes you desperately want to be seen by. We build castles in our minds for people who will never even walk past the gates. We convince ourselves that if we just love them quietly enough, purely enough, eventually they will notice. But the world doesn't work like that. Love without reciprocation isn't a romantic tragedy; it is just a quiet, exhausting room where you are always the only one speaking.

When I started to understand how people decide whether to give their attention or not, I realized something dark about the world. Love has, in many ways, lost its deeper meaning; it is often seen through a purely materialistic lens. People decide whether to stay or not based on how you appear, how you present yourself, how seamlessly you fit into their expectations. This is not just between a boy and a girl, but with everyone. If you look good, if you fit the mold, people stay. If you don't, they move away. I am not saying that having preferences is entirely wrong, because nature works that way. But humans have exceeded the boundaries of what nature intended.

And what happens to those who don't fit the mold? Those of us who carry our love like a heavy, unpolished stone? We are left to wander the catacombs of our own minds. I have buried my anguish like a coward, thinking that silence was strength. But silence is a tomb, and I became its sole inhabitant. I learned the hard way that you cannot force someone to find you beautiful, and you cannot love someone into loving you back. You can only stand there, hands bleeding from the thorns, offering them a flower they never asked for.

If you say that the world has not moved beyond nature, then explain to me the symbiosis of human love. How is a person capable of killing themselves because their counterpart died or left them? This isn't in nature. The primary instinct of any organism is to survive. Why would a human destroy themselves for love? And yet, it happens every day.

Tell me what this means. It means that true companionship is the only thing that keeps a life from descending into absolute misery. Because if you have a companion, you can share the unbearable weight of what you are going through with someone you trust—someone you know will not turn their back and walk away.

And the whole point of this is simple.
We see people.
But we do not feel what they feel. We observe their suffering, but we refuse to step inside it.

The journey into the depths of human longing continues...
